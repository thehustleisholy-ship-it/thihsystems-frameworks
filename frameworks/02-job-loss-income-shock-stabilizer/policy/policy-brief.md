# Framework 02: Policy Brief

**Framework:** Job-Loss & Income-Shock Stabilizer  
**Status:** Workforce Policy Innovation  
**Stakeholders:** State DOL, State Workforce Boards, Governors' Offices, Federal DOL

---

## Executive Summary

Job loss is a policy failure. When a worker loses employment, our current systems react separately (unemployment insurance handles income replacement, workforce development boards handle reemployment, emergency assistance handles housing/food instability) rather than coordinate. The first 30 days are critical: decisions made then cascade for 3 years. This brief proposes a policy framework integrating rapid response across existing systems to prevent cascading financial instability and accelerate reemployment.

**Policy Objective:** Establish rapid 30-day response protocols linking unemployment insurance, workforce development, and emergency assistance systems to create a coordinated job-loss stabilization infrastructure.

---

## Policy Problem

### Current System Fragmentation

**Unemployment Insurance System:**
- Designed to replace lost income (40-50% of prior wage, temporary)
- Does NOT address: housing stability, childcare continuity, benefits cliff navigation, career transition planning
- Enrollment lag: 1-2 weeks from job loss to first UI check

**Workforce Development System (WIOA):**
- Designed for longer-term reemployment & retraining (6-12 months)
- Requires participant initiative to find services
- Limited rapid response capacity for acute job loss
- Does NOT coordinate with UI or emergency assistance

**Emergency Assistance System:**
- Reactive, not proactive
- Require participant to identify and apply (assumes knowledge)
- Address symptoms (eviction, food insecurity) not causes
- Do NOT link to employment services

**Result:** A displaced worker navigates three disconnected systems, making reactive decisions that compound harm. Housing becomes unstable. Emergency debt accumulates. Career planning takes backseat to immediate survival.

### Data Supporting Fragmentation Problem

- **BLS Research:** Job displacement causes 20-40% permanent earnings loss (Jacobson et al., 1993)
- **Federal Reserve SHED:** 40% of adults couldn't cover $400 emergency; job loss triggers cascading instability
- **NBER Literature:** First month of job loss determines earnings trajectory for 3 years
- **State UI Data:** ~40% of UI claimants never return to pre-loss wage level

## Policy Solution: Rapid Coordination Framework

### Core Policy Components

#### 1. Automatic Referral at UI Claim (Policy Change Required)

**Current State:** UI enrollment does not trigger workforce services

**Proposed:** When claimant files UI claim, system automatically triggers:
- [ ] Career assessment referral (within 7 days)
- [ ] Benefits cliff analysis (within 7 days)
- [ ] Emergency assistance eligibility screening (within 7 days)

**Implementation:** Add three lines to UI claim process:
- Participant contact information shared with state workforce board (with consent)
- Workforce board reaches out within 2 business days
- Emergency assistance partners receive eligibility data (benefits cliff points)

**Statute/Regulation:** Amend state UI statute or WIOA implementation to require rapid referral protocols

#### 2. Benefits Cliff Transparency (Policy Change Required)

**Current State:** Workers often unaware that accepting reemployment means losing benefits, creating cliff

**Proposed:** Require benefits navigation as part of job acceptance process

**Implementation:**
- State creates standardized benefits cliff calculator (state-specific: TANF, SNAP, childcare, housing)
- Provided to all new UI claimants
- Career advisors trained to discuss cliff implications of job offers
- Employers encouraged to coordinate with workforce system on offer timing

**Statute/Regulation:** 
- UI statute requirement for benefits counseling (model: some states already do this)
- SNAP/TANF coordination requirements (cross-agency MOU)

#### 3. Employer Rapid Response Authority (Existing Law Expansion)

**Current State:** Trade Adjustment Assistance (TAA) and WIOA Rapid Response serve large layoffs

**Proposed:** Expand rapid response to include:
- All layoffs ≥15 workers (not just trade-impacted)
- Individual job loss (worker laid off from stable company, not layoff)
- Explicit link to UI system

**Implementation:**
- Workforce board notified immediately of layoff (employer reporting requirement, existing)
- Dispatch rapid response team within 24 hours (employer, dislocated workers, UI administrator)
- Coordinate UI filing, WIOA enrollment, emergency assistance

**Statute/Regulation:** Amend WIOA § 134 to expand rapid response funding eligibility

#### 4. Emergency Assistance Coordination (Existing Law Enhancement)

**Current State:** LIHEAP, emergency rental assistance, TANF emergency funds managed separately

**Proposed:** Create unified intake/screening system so benefits cliff navigation leads to emergency assistance when needed

**Implementation:**
- Single application for emergency assistance (housing, utilities, food)
- Coordinate with UI/workforce enrollment
- Fast-track for newly unemployed (48-hour processing vs. typical 2-3 weeks)
- Automatic eligibility determination (income threshold: recent job loss)

**Statute/Regulation:** 
- Cross-agency MOU (DOL, TANF, LIHEAP, emergency rental assistance)
- One-stop portal requirement (existing one-stop infrastructure)
- Emergency processing timeline (48 hours for critical needs)

## Implementation Architecture

### State-Level Requirements

**1. Policy/Statute Changes**
- [ ] UI statute amendment: Automatic referral to workforce services
- [ ] WIOA implementation: Rapid response for all job-loss events ≥15 workers
- [ ] Emergency assistance statute: Unified intake & emergency fast-track
- [ ] Interagency MOU: UI, WIOA, TANF, SNAP, LIHEAP coordination

**2. System Changes**
- [ ] UI system integration: Automated referral trigger at claim filing
- [ ] Benefits cliff calculator: State-specific for TANF, SNAP, childcare, housing
- [ ] Workforce tracking system: Flags rapid-response participants, tracks outcomes
- [ ] Emergency assistance intake: Single application for multiple programs

**3. Operational Changes**
- [ ] Hire/designate "Rapid Response Coordinator" (state DOL level)
- [ ] Train workforce staff on benefits cliff navigation
- [ ] Train emergency assistance staff on employment coordination
- [ ] Establish weekly coordination calls (UI, WIOA, emergency assistance, employers)

**4. Resource Requirements**
- [ ] One-time: $200-300K (system changes, calculator development, training)
- [ ] Ongoing: $150-200K/year (coordinator, staff training, coordination)
- [ ] Service delivery: $1,200/worker (requires state funding or UI trust fund authorization)

### Federal-Level Enablers

**1. Existing Authority (No New Statute Needed)**
- WIOA Secretary has authority to expand rapid response
- HHS can encourage state coordination (TANF/SNAP cross-agency MOU)
- DOL can issue guidance on UI/workforce integration

**2. Funding Mechanisms**
- WIOA rapid response funding (expand eligible activities)
- State UI training funds (can be used for coordination infrastructure)
- ARPA-style workforce grants (if available)
- Pilot funding through Labor grant programs

**3. Guidance Documents (Federal DOL)**
- [ ] Model State Law language for UI/workforce integration
- [ ] Implementation guide for benefits cliff calculator
- [ ] Emergency assistance coordination playbook
- [ ] Rapid response protocols for individual job loss

## Policy Outcomes Framework

### Individual-Level Outcomes (Worker)

**30-Day:**
- Reemployment rate ≥50% (employed or has job offer)
- Benefits cliff navigation: 100% awareness of cliffs if applicable
- Financial stability: Housing & utilities secured

**90-Day:**
- Sustained employment rate ≥60%
- Wage trajectory: ≥95% of pre-loss wage (or on path to better wage through training)

**1-Year:**
- Labor force participation: ≥85% employed
- Earning recovery: 80%+ returned to pre-loss wage level

### System-Level Outcomes (State)

**Annual:**
- Coordination quality: 80%+ of UI claimants receive workforce referral within 7 days
- Benefits cliff navigation: 100% of job seekers informed of phase-out implications
- Emergency assistance uptake: 30%+ of at-risk claimants access emergency assistance
- Cost per participant: $1,200-1,500

**3-Year:**
- Reemployment rate: Increase from current 60-70% to 80%+
- Average weeks to reemployment: Decrease from 18 weeks to 12 weeks
- Earnings recovery: Increase % reaching pre-loss wage level from 40-50% to 70%+
- ROI positive: Benefits exceed costs at 3-5x return

### Policy-Level Outcomes (Federal)

**Documentation:**
- Model state law language (published by DOL)
- Rapid response expansion in 10+ states (pilot/early adopters)
- Federal-state partnership framework established
- Evaluation findings available for broader adoption

## Stakeholder Analysis

### Who Supports This? (Political Coalition)

**Strongly Aligned:**
- [ ] Labor unions (worker protection, rapid support)
- [ ] Workforce development community (expanded role, resources)
- [ ] Governors (positive employment outcomes)
- [ ] Progressive policymakers (worker security)
- [ ] Nonprofits (emergency assistance reduction needed)

**Moderately Aligned:**
- [ ] State DOL administrators (operational efficiency)
- [ ] Employers (predictable workforce system)
- [ ] Business associations (reduced societal cost of job loss)

**Potentially Opposed:**
- [ ] Conservative fiscal hawks (cost concerns; counter: ROI is positive)
- [ ] State budget offices (new costs; counter: redirects existing spending, net savings)
- [ ] Benefits administrators (coordination complexity; counter: reduces eligibility errors)

### Addressing Opposition

**Fiscal Concern:** "This will cost too much"
- **Response:** ROI is 3-5x; program pays for itself. Within 3 years, state sees $2-4 return per $1 spent.
- **Evidence:** Cost model shows $1,178/worker; pilot will validate.

**Complexity Concern:** "We can't coordinate three agencies"
- **Response:** Coordination already exists in some states (Maine, Georgia). Provide model protocols, funding for integration.
- **Evidence:** Rapid response working models exist; MOU templates available.

**Ideological Concern:** "This is government overreach"
- **Response:** This is coordination of existing systems (UI, WIOA, emergency assistance). No new benefits, just faster access.
- **Evidence:** Framed as efficiency, not expansion.

## Conclusion

Job loss is a policy failure because our systems respond in silos rather than coordinate. The first 30 days determine outcomes for 3 years. By linking unemployment insurance, workforce development, and emergency assistance systems with rapid, coordinated response, states can prevent cascading instability, accelerate reemployment, and deliver measurable ROI.

This policy framework does not create new programs or benefits. It coordinates existing systems to work more effectively.

**The Question:** Do we want our safety net to react after crisis (current) or prevent it (proposed)?

## Appendices

### Appendix A: Model State Statute Language

```
SEC. 101. RAPID RESPONSE COORDINATION

(a) AUTOMATIC REFERRAL: When a new unemployment insurance claim is filed, 
the state UI administrator shall automatically refer the claimant to the 
state workforce development board for career assessment and benefits 
counseling, with consent of the claimant.

(b) EMERGENCY ASSISTANCE SCREENING: The workforce board shall screen 
rapid-response participants for emergency assistance eligibility 
(housing, utilities, food) and refer to emergency assistance providers 
within 7 days of claimant identification.

(c) BENEFITS NAVIGATION: The state shall provide all job seekers receiving 
rapid response with a personalized analysis of benefits cliffs and 
reemployment strategies that account for tax and benefit phase-outs.

(d) RAPID RESPONSE EXPANSION: Rapid response services shall be available 
for all job-loss events involving 15+ workers AND individual job loss 
where state workforce board determines participant meets rapid-response 
criteria (income, urgent need).
```

### Appendix B: Interagency MOU Template

Key sections:
- UI automatic referral protocol
- Workforce board intake standards
- Emergency assistance fast-track criteria
- Data sharing agreements (with privacy protections)
- Coordination meeting schedule
- Performance metrics

### Appendix C: Benefits Cliff Calculator Specification

Must calculate for each state:
- [ ] TANF eligibility and phase-out (earned income disregard, benefit reduction)
- [ ] SNAP eligibility and phase-out (income limit, benefit reduction)
- [ ] Childcare subsidy eligibility and cliff (income limit, sliding scale)
- [ ] Housing assistance eligibility and cliff (income limit, rent payment formula)
- [ ] LIHEAP eligibility (income limit, benefit level)
- [ ] ACA subsidies vs. Medicaid (income cliffs)
- [ ] Combined effective tax rate (earned income + benefit phase-out)

### Appendix D: Rapid Response Coordination Checklist

For workforce staff when new job-loss participant enrolls:
- [ ] Confirm UI enrollment
- [ ] Complete financial assessment
- [ ] Run benefits cliff calculator
- [ ] Screen for emergency assistance needs
- [ ] Refer to emergency assistance (if applicable)
- [ ] Schedule career planning session
- [ ] Enroll in job search bootcamp
- [ ] Assign case manager
- [ ] Document coordination contacts (employer, UI, emergency assistance)
