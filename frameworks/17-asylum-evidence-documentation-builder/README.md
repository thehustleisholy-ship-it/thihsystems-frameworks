# Framework 17: Asylum Evidence & Documentation Builder

**Strategic anchor:** A person should not lose the ability to communicate the truth merely because crisis, displacement, trauma, or fragmented records made that truth difficult to organize.

This is a researched public-interest concept and deterministic educational illustration—not a THIHsystems-operated legal product. It does not accept documents or personal narratives. It never determines eligibility, predicts outcomes, assesses credibility, generates testimony, fills factual gaps, erases inconsistencies, gives individualized legal advice, or submits a filing.

## Human pain and documented need

People may need to reconstruct events while records sit across devices, accounts, languages, countries, and trusted contacts. Current Form I-589 instructions ask for reasonably available corroborative evidence, explanations when supporting evidence is not reasonably available, and full certified English translations of foreign-language documents. Those are legal-process facts, not a claim that documentary completeness proves truth.

Peer-reviewed research links trauma and some mental-health symptoms with difficulty retrieving specific autobiographical memories, but findings vary. An inconsistency is not automatically evidence of fabrication. This framework therefore stores uncertainty explicitly (`exact`, `approximate`, `unknown`, `conflicted`) and never “repairs” a narrative.

## Proposed AI role

- classify user-confirmed items into a document inventory;
- identify empty provenance fields and translation queues;
- surface, never reconcile, date or transliteration conflicts;
- format a person-approved summary of what is known, unknown, and unresolved;
- route legal questions to a qualified attorney or accredited representative.

AI must not infer facts, score credibility, decide relevance to legal elements, coach phrasing, generate declarations, recommend consistency, or treat country-condition material as proof of an individual experience.

## Privacy and safety

A real continuation would need data minimization, informed and revocable consent, threat modeling for device/account monitoring, encryption in transit and at rest, role-based access, short retention, verifiable deletion, safe export, breach response, and explicit rules for third-party/witness data. “Local” or “encrypted” alone would not establish safety.

## Invitation

People with lived experience, immigration attorneys, EOIR-accredited representatives, interpreters, advocates, trauma researchers, and privacy/security practitioners are invited to correct the claims, boundaries, language, and data model—or continue the idea independently. No institutional adoption or pilot is requested.
