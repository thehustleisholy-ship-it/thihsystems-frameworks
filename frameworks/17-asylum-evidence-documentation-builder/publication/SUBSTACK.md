# When the Truth Is Real but the Record Is Incomplete

*A responsible AI framework for helping asylum seekers organize timelines, documents, experiences, and unresolved evidence before speaking with qualified legal counsel.*

## The person behind the paperwork

A person can know what happened and still struggle to assemble the record. A date may be approximate. A message may remain on a lost phone. A name may be transliterated three ways. A document may be held by someone who would be endangered by contact. Retelling may itself carry a cost.

The humane starting point is simple: truth and record completeness are not the same thing.

## Why a truthful experience can still be difficult to document

Displacement fragments records. Translation adds another layer of meaning and verification. Trauma and some mental-health symptoms can affect autobiographical recall for some people, although research is heterogeneous and no diagnosis or memory pattern proves or disproves an account. Repeated interviews can produce discrepancies even without fabrication. These cautions do not decide any individual case; they explain why a system must preserve uncertainty instead of treating it as a software defect.

## The hidden system failure

Most tools reward clean fields: one date, one spelling, one version. Human experience is not always delivered that way. A form optimized for completion can pressure a person to guess. A generative system optimized for coherence can quietly turn an unknown into a sentence that sounds certain. In this setting, fluency can be dangerous.

Current USCIS Form I-589 instructions address reasonably available corroborative evidence, explanations when evidence is not reasonably available, and certified English translations. The regulation at 8 CFR 208.13(a) also states that credible testimony may be sufficient without corroboration. Those rules belong in qualified legal hands. They do not authorize software to decide what evidence is enough.

## What better preparation could make possible

A safer preparation layer could show what exists, where it came from, what language it is in, what remains unavailable, which dates conflict, and which questions need counsel. It could reduce the burden of searching across scattered sources without rewriting the person’s account.

## Where AI could assist—and where it must stop

AI might sort user-confirmed items, identify empty provenance fields, build a translation queue, and format an approved inventory. It must never infer missing facts, assess credibility, determine eligibility, predict approval, coach testimony, erase inconsistencies, offer individualized legal advice, or submit a filing.

Legal advice and representation should come from authorized practitioners. EOIR guidance warns that notarios, document preparers, consultants, and travel agents cannot provide immigration legal advice or representation.

## The proposed interactive illustration

Framework 17 includes a no-upload, deterministic illustration using only three clearly labeled synthetic scenarios. Sliders represent chronology coverage, document categories, translation needs, provenance, conflicts, missing-evidence explanations, legal-review status, and secure-export review. Its percentage is merely an interface orientation signal. It has no legal meaning.

The output is a completeness map, visible conflict list, translation queue, inventory count, counsel-conversation questions, and privacy warning. There is no case narrative field, credibility score, eligibility result, filing recommendation, or agency connector.

## Privacy, safety, and legal limitations

Real asylum records can expose applicants, relatives, witnesses, and advocates. A responsible continuation would require data minimization, threat modeling, revocable consent, encryption, access control, short retention, provable deletion, breach response, and safe-device guidance. “Encrypted” is not enough if an unsafe person controls the device or account.

This framework is educational, not legal advice, not a legal service, and not a product operated by THIHsystems. Legal requirements change and vary by posture and forum. A qualified immigration attorney or properly accredited representative must review an individual situation.

## What remains unresolved

The hardest questions are not interface questions: when should intake stop; what should never be collected; how can deletion be proved; how should alternate calendars and transliterations be represented; and how can third-party information be protected? The open-questions register remains part of the work, not an appendix to ignore.

Immigration attorneys, accredited representatives, interpreters, advocates, researchers, privacy practitioners, and people with lived experience are invited to correct, improve, or independently continue this framework.

How many truthful experiences become harder to understand because the person lived through the crisis but never had a safe way to organize the record?
