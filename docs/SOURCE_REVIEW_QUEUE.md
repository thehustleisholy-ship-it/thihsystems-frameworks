## Notes on Sourcing

### What Counts as a Source
- Peer-reviewed academic research
- Government agency data (CDC, USGS, BLS, USDA, EPA, etc.)
- Official institutional reports (World Bank, OECD, etc.)
- Published books with citations
- Nonprofit research reports (with methodology disclosed)

### What Does NOT Count
- Blog posts or medium articles without citations
- Consultancy reports without disclosed methodology
- Anecdotal stories or "trust me"
- Wikipedia (cite the sources Wikipedia cites instead)
- Marketing copy or sales presentations
- Uncited news stories

### When to Mark "Needs Softening"
If a claim is true but overstated:
- Instead of: "The world is running out of sand" → "Rapid sand extraction in some regions is outpacing replenishment"
- Instead of: "Phosphorus is routinely wasted" → "A significant fraction of phosphorus is lost to aquatic systems"

### When to Mark "Do Not Publish Yet"
If a claim is speculative or unsourced:
- "Longer lives will break pension systems" (true possibility, but unsourced)
- "Geoengineering will cause crop failure" (plausible concern, but speculative)

These don't go public until either sourced or reframed as scenarios.
